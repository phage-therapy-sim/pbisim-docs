Tutorial 05 — Phage Pharmacokinetics¶

Prerequisites: Tutorials 01–04

In tutorials 01–04, phage doses were applied directly to the infection site (P). This is appropriate for local delivery (topical application, nebulisation into a lung cavity). When phage are given intravenously (IV) — the route used in most systemic phage therapy — they must first distribute from the blood to the infection site, a process governed by pharmacokinetics (PK).

What you will learn

  1. Why IV phage PK is needed and what the two compartments represent
  2. Effect-compartment mode: blood concentration drives site delivery (one-way)
  3. Mass-conserving mode (Vi): bidirectional; total phage count conserved
  4. k_elim: phage clearance from blood (innate immune system, filtration)
  5. k_back: lysis-driven phage re-entry into blood — why infected patients maintain higher blood titres than uninfected controls
  6. Peripheral tissue compartment (k12, k21)
  7. How to choose between the two modes

1 Imports and shared bacterial model¶

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

from pbisim import (ModelBuilder, PBIModel, solve_ode,
                    PhagePKConfig, DoseEvent, DoseSchedule)

# Single-strain bacterial model — same parameters as Tutorial 01
# (no phage PK yet; we will add PK layer progressively)
BASE = (
    ModelBuilder(n_bacteria=1, n_phages=1, n_latent=5)
    .with_growth_rates(1.0, bacteria_to_resource_ratio=1e9)
    .with_phage_params(
        adsorption_rates    = 2e-9,   # mL h⁻¹
        burst_sizes         = 50.0,   # PFU per lysed cell
        latent_periods      = 0.5,    # h
        phage_decay_rates   = 0.1,    # h⁻¹ intrinsic phage decay at site
    )
    .with_nutrient(monod_constant=0.3, recycle_fraction=0.0)
)

B0 = np.array([1e7])    # initial bacteria (cells/mL)
T_END = 72.0            # h

# Phage dose as AMOUNT to central compartment (Pc = phage count in blood)
# For effect-compartment and Vi modes:  DoseEvent adds to Pc.
# For direct mode (no PK):             DoseEvent adds to P directly.
DOSE_AMOUNT = 1e12      # phage administered IV (total phage count)

2 Mode 0 — Direct dosing (no PK, local delivery)¶

Without PhagePKConfig, a dose event with target="phage" adds directly to the infection-site concentration P (PFU / mL). This is appropriate for phage applied locally (wound irrigation, inhaled aerosol).

The infection site immediately has high phage density → rapid killing from t = 0.

# Direct: dose (in PFU/mL) goes straight to P at t = 0

sched_direct = DoseSchedule([
    DoseEvent(time=0, amount=direct_dose_mL, target="phage", index=0)

cfg_direct = BASE.with_dose_schedule(sched_direct).build()
res_direct = solve_ode(
    PBIModel(cfg_direct, initial_B=B0, initial_P=np.array([0.0])),
    t_end=T_END, dt=0.25,
)
print(f"Peak site phage (direct):  {res_direct.get('P0').max():.2e} PFU/mL at t=0+")
print(f"Bacteria at 48h (direct):  {res_direct.get('B0')[-2]:.2e} cells/mL")

3 Mode 1 — Effect-compartment PK (Vi = None)¶

With PhagePKConfig and Vi = None (default), the central compartment Pc (phage count in blood) drives delivery to the infection site via:

dP/dt  += k_in · Cc  −  k_out · P       Cc = Pc / Vc (blood concentration)
dPc/dt  = R_dose(t)  −  k_elim · Pc     (no feedback from P back to Pc)

Biological interpretation of parameters:

Parameter Typical value Biological meaning
k_elim 0.05–0.3 h⁻¹ Blood clearance: innate immunity, liver, kidney; phage half-life ≈ 3–14 h
k_in 0.1–1 h⁻¹ Rate at which blood phage concentration drives accumulation at the site
k_out 0.1–1 h⁻¹ Site clearance: local innate immunity, phage decay
Vc 5 L Blood volume (adult); scales the blood concentration Cc = Pc/Vc

At steady state (no biology): P* = (k_in / k_out) · Cc

In [3]:
ppk_effect = PhagePKConfig(
    n_phages    = 1,
    k_elim      = np.array([0.1]),    # h⁻¹ — phage half-life in blood ≈ 7h
    k_in        = np.array([0.3]),    # h⁻¹ — delivery rate to infection site
    k_out       = np.array([0.2]),    # h⁻¹ — clearance at infection site
    Vc          = np.array([5000.0]), # mL  — adult blood volume (5 L)
)

# Dose: add DOSE_AMOUNT phage to Pc (blood) at t=0
sched_iv = DoseSchedule([
    DoseEvent(time=0, amount=DOSE_AMOUNT, target="phage", index=0)
])
cfg_effect = BASE.with_phage_pk(ppk_effect).with_dose_schedule(sched_iv).build()
res_effect = solve_ode(
    PBIModel(cfg_effect, initial_B=B0,
             initial_P=np.array([0.0]),
             initial_Pc=np.array([0.0])),   # phage enters via dose schedule
    t_end=T_END, dt=0.25,
)

Cc_effect = res_effect.get("Pc0") / ppk_effect.Vc[0]    # blood concentration
print(f"Peak blood conc (Cc):  {Cc_effect.max():.2e} PFU/mL at t=0+")
print(f"Peak site conc (P):    {res_effect.get('P0').max():.2e} PFU/mL")
print(f"Bacteria at 48h:       {res_effect.get('B0')[-2]:.2e} cells/mL")

4 Direct vs effect-compartment: route of delivery shapes site kinetics¶

Direct dosing delivers phage immediately to the infection site at full therapeutic concentration. There is no reservoir — once those phage adsorb or decay, they are gone — but the initial concentration can be very high, enabling rapid bacterial killing before resistance can develop.

IV dosing with effect-compartment PK deposits phage into the blood, from which they distribute to the infection site at rate k_in · Cc. This means:

  • Site concentration starts at zero and builds up gradually
  • Peak site concentration is lower than with direct dosing for the same total dose
  • As blood phage are cleared (k_elim), the delivery rate to the site also declines

Because bacteria experience lower phage pressure early in the IV arm, there is more time for resistant mutants to be selected — visible as earlier resistance emergence in the simulation below.

Clinical implication: IV delivery is not preferred because it is more potent at the infection site; it is used when direct (local) delivery is not practical (deep-tissue infections, systemic bacteraemia). For accessible sites, local delivery (wound irrigation, inhaled aerosol) achieves higher site concentrations from the same total dose.

5 Mass-conserving mode (Vi) — bidirectional transfer¶

When Vi is provided, the k_in/k_out transfer terms are bidirectional and conserve phage between the two compartments:

dPc/dt += −k_in · Pc  +  k_out · P · Vi    (transfer terms only)
  dP/dt +=  k_in · Pc / Vi  −  k_out · P

The full equations also include −k_elim · Pc (blood clearance) and, once bacteria are present, the lysis burst adds phage directly to P. Therefore Pc + P · Vi is not a strict constant in a biological simulation:

  • k_elim removes phage from blood → total decreases
  • Phage replication (lysis burst at infection site) adds phage to P → total increases

The "mass-conserving" label refers to the bidirectional transfer geometry, not to global phage count conservation.

Use this mode when the infection-site volume is known (e.g., a 5–20 mL abscess cavity) and you need the two-way exchange to be physically accurate. The effect-compartment approximation is appropriate when site volume is negligible relative to blood volume or is uncertain.

Steady-state site concentration (no biology, no k_elim): P* = k_in · Pc / (k_out · Vi)

Parameter calibration warning: k_in has different physical meanings in the two modes.

Mode Site delivery rate Steady-state P
Effect compartment k_in · Pc/Vc (k_in/k_out) · Pc/Vc
Mass-conserving (Vi) k_in · Pc/Vi (k_in/k_out) · Pc/Vi

With Vc = 5000 mL and Vi = 20 mL, using the same k_in = 0.3 would deliver 250× more phage to the site in Vi mode — making the comparison unfair. To match the same initial delivery rate, scale by the volume ratio: k_in_vi = k_in_eff × (Vi / Vc)

In [5]:
Vi_val = 20.0   # mL — 20 mL abscess cavity

# Scale k_in so that the initial phage delivery rate (PFU/mL/h at site)
# matches the effect-compartment mode.
# Effect-comp delivers k_in * Pc/Vc; Vi-mode delivers k_in * Pc/Vi.
# Setting k_in_vi = k_in_eff * Vi/Vc equalises both rates.
k_in_vi = ppk_effect.k_in[0] * Vi_val / ppk_effect.Vc[0]   # 0.3 × 20/5000 = 0.0012
print(f"k_in (effect-comp) = {ppk_effect.k_in[0]:.4f},  "
      f"k_in_vi (Vi-mode, scaled) = {k_in_vi:.6f}")

ppk_vi = PhagePKConfig(
    n_phages = 1,
    k_elim   = np.array([0.1]),
    k_in     = np.array([k_in_vi]),   # scaled for equivalent phage delivery rate
    k_out    = np.array([0.2]),
    Vc       = np.array([5000.0]),
    Vi       = np.array([Vi_val]),     # infection-site volume (mL)
)
cfg_vi = BASE.with_phage_pk(ppk_vi).with_dose_schedule(sched_iv).build()
res_vi = solve_ode(
    PBIModel(cfg_vi, initial_B=B0,
             initial_P=np.array([0.0]),
             initial_Pc=np.array([0.0])),
    t_end=T_END, dt=0.25,
)

# Verify conservation: Pc + P*Vi should be constant after dose at t=0+
Pc_vi = res_vi.get("Pc0")
P_vi  = res_vi.get("P0")

print(f"Initial total phage (t≈0): {total[1]:.3e}")
print(f"Final   total phage (t={T_END}h): {total[-1]:.3e}")
pct = (total[-1] - total[1]) / total[1] * 100

6 k_back — lysis-driven phage re-entry into blood¶

A striking clinical observation: phage concentrations in the blood of infected patients treated with IV phage are higher and persist longer than in uninfected controls given the same dose. The explanation is that phage released by bacterial lysis at the infection site spill back into the bloodstream.

k_back (h⁻¹) adds a drain from P back to blood:

dP/dt  −= k_back · P
dPc/dt += k_back · P · Vi    (mass-conserving with Vi)

The spill-back rate is proportional to the site phage concentration, which grows when bacteria are actively being lysed. High k_back therefore elevates and sustains blood titres throughout the therapeutic period, while slightly reducing site concentration.

7 k_in / k_out ratio — the site-to-blood equilibrium¶

At pseudo-steady state (slow Pc depletion), site concentration approaches: P* ≈ (k_in / k_out) · Pc / Vi

A high k_in/k_out ratio concentrates phage at the site (favourable for deep-tissue or biofilm infections). A low ratio means phage are cleared from the site faster than they are delivered — equivalent to a short effective half-life at the site.

Biologically, k_out reflects local innate immune clearance and mucociliary transport; k_in depends on tissue perfusion and phage diffusivity.

8 Summary¶

Mode Vi Site equation Use when
No PK — dose → P directly Local/topical delivery
Effect compartment None dP += k_in·Cc − k_out·P Site volume unknown/negligible
Mass-conserving array dP += k_in·Pc/Vi − k_out·P Known site volume; bidirectional exchange needed

Pc is the phage amount (total count) in blood; Cc = Pc/Vc is the blood concentration. DoseEvent(target="phage") adds to Pc when PK is configured and to P directly otherwise.

Key clinical insights:

  • Local delivery achieves higher immediate site concentrations than IV for the same total dose. IV is used when direct access to the infection site is not clinically practical.
  • The k_in/k_out ratio determines how efficiently blood phage accumulate at the infection site.
  • k_back explains why infected patients treated with IV phage maintain higher and more durable blood titres than uninfected controls: phage released by bacterial lysis at the site spill back into blood.
